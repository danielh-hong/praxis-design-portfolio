My Initial Engineering Position:

In January, my position as an engineering designer was deeply rooted in a philosophy of perfectionism. Still attached to my high school views, I held a deeply rooted internal belief that all the things I did, and all the products I created, had to be of seamless functionality, flawless design, and unparalleled aesthetics.

At the time, I did not realize this; I thought I had let go of my high school ideals ‚Äî I thought I was beyond perfectionism. In my initial position statement, I even talked about discipline, repetition, teamwork, and structure, even explicitly reasoning with myself to not include perfectionism, as I had grown out of it.

However, now in April, 4 months later, I see in hindsight that those values of discipline, repetition, teamwork, and structure were just the tangible realizations of my perfectionism. They were concrete expressions that supported my illusion of having transcended perfectionism, when, in fact, I was still very much stuck in the middle of it. This perspective, while thorough and not fundamentally "bad", often prioritized flawless execution over all else, which impacted both the speed of development of products and designs, and also often the creative scope.



Initial Engineering Philosophy:

  • Perfection in Execution: creating error free designs that work in every front. Designs that are both detailed, yet functional, with consistent performance.
  • Detail-Oriented Design: focusing on every minor aspect of designs in order to achieve high standards.
  • Being Critical: having highly realistic standards, often disregarding more creative, less feasible designs.
  • Stable, Solid Solutions: prioritizing solutions that were realistic to complete in a complete manner in the project time frame, without daring to dream large.

My Evolution in Position:

My second semester in the Engineering Science program has brought a lot of changes to my life. Taking 6 courses, all of which I no longer have prior knowledge, joining two design teams, taking on my own little projects, balancing health, and getting exposure to actual engineering design has challenged me to rethink the practical implications of perfectionism, and to recognize the limitations they were placing on me in terms of innovation and actual design.

Specifically, taking on actual engineering projects, conversing with other students and professors, and learning about the the engineering process in Praxis has made me realize that my fundamental value in perfectionism was hindering me, as it caused me to strive for stable, solid solutions and products that I thought could be perfect on all fronts rather than outside the box, unbelievable solutions.

I have come to believe now that the essence of engineering isn't found in the flawless execution of an idea but rather in the boldness of the concept itself. True innovation requires stepping beyond the bounds of conventional perfection to embrace the potential of groundbreaking ideas, and accepting that along the way, there will be countless stumbles, trips, and bruises. I now see that if the final product doesn't work on all fronts; that's okay. Now, I truly believe that It's the idea that matters.



Current Engineering Philosophy:

Today, my design philosophy is driven by an "innovation-first" approach. My new position emphasizes:

  • Valuing Visionary Ideas: Prioritizing the pursuit of ambitious, even seemingly unreachable goals, as idea is key.
  • Thinking Outside the Box: Encouraging unconventional thinking, "unbelievable aspects", that challenges the acceptable and the common, knowing that while perfection may not always be achievable, the pursuit itself and unconventional thinking can lead to extraordinary developments.
  • Embracing the Art of the Possible: Focusing on what could be, rather than what already is‚Äîpushing the limits of current technologies and methodologies to explore new possibilities.
  • Dreaming Big: Pursuing ambitious ideas. Believing that by reaching high, even if the final product doesn't work on all fronts, the journey and the idea itself is what matters.
  • Being open to all possibilities; you never can fully know whether something will work or not until you try it.

This new approach and position is not about forgoing precision or quality but about recalibrating the balance between perfection and innovation. It's about understanding that while the path to an ideal solution is important, the real impact often lies in daring to imagine and attempting the extraordinary. It's about utilizing my strengths in creativity and outside-the-box thinking to create meaningful ideas that can might, one day, be accepted into this quickly advancing world.

Now, to me, It's about thinking unconventionally. It's about being creative. It's about pursuing the ecentric ideas. It's about embracing the difficulty.
